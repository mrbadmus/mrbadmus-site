⚡ Weight, Mass and Gravity

Spec 4.5.1 📙 Higher
📖 In-Depth Theory

Mass vs Weight — The Classic Confusion

Mass is the amount of matter in an object — measured in kg.
Mass never changes, regardless of where you are (Earth, Moon, space).
Weight is the gravitational force on an object — measured in Newtons (N).
Weight DOES change depending on the gravitational field strength of where you are.

Gravitational Field Strength (g)

g is the pull of gravity per kilogram of mass.
On Earth: g ≈ 10 N/kg (sometimes given as 9.8 N/kg — use whatever the question gives).
On the Moon: g ≈ 1.6 N/kg — much weaker gravity.
On Jupiter: g ≈ 25 N/kg — much stronger gravity.
A 70 kg person weighs 700 N on Earth, 112 N on the Moon, 1750 N on Jupiter.

Centre of Mass

The centre of mass is the single point where all of an object's weight seems to act.
For uniform shapes, it's the geometric centre (middle of a ruler, centre of a ball).
An object is stable if its centre of mass is above its base.
Low centre of mass + wide base = very stable (why racing cars are wide and low).
⚠️ Common Mistake

Mass and weight are NOT the same. Mass is in kg, weight is in Newtons. Never write 'my weight is 60 kg' — that's your mass! Weight = 60 × 10 = 600 N.

📐 Variables
WWeight (W) is measured in Newtons (N)
mMass (m) is measured in Kilograms (kg)
gGravitational field strength (g) is measured in N/kg (N/kg)
📐 Key Equations
W = mg
📌 Key Note

g on Earth = 10 N/kg. g on Moon ≈ 1.6 N/kg. Weight (N) = Mass (kg) × g (N/kg).

⚽ FIFA Worked Examples
Example 1 — Calculate weight on Earth

A student has a mass of 55 kg. Calculate their weight on Earth. (g = 10 N/kg)

F

W = m × g

I

W = 55 × 10

F

No unit conversion needed

A

W = 550 N

Example 2 — Weight on the Moon

The same student visits the Moon where g = 1.6 N/kg. Calculate their weight on the Moon.

F

W = m × g

I

W = 55 × 1.6

F

Mass stays the same — only g changes

A

W = 88 N
