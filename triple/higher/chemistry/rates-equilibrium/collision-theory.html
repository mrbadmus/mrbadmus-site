Collision Theory and Activation Energy

Spec 5.6.1.3 📙 Higher
📖 In-Depth Theory

Collision Theory

COLLISION THEORY explains why reactions happen and what affects their rate.
For a reaction to occur:
1. Reactant particles must COLLIDE with each other.
2. The collision must have sufficient ENERGY — equal to or greater than the ACTIVATION ENERGY.
3. The particles must be in the CORRECT ORIENTATION (for some reactions).
Most collisions are UNSUCCESSFUL — the particles collide but bounce off without reacting, because they don't have enough energy.
Only EFFECTIVE (successful) collisions lead to products.
This explains all rate factors:
Higher temperature → faster particles → more collisions AND more above activation energy → more effective collisions.
Higher concentration → more particles per volume → more collisions → more effective collisions.
Larger surface area → more exposed particles → more collisions → more effective collisions.

Activation Energy

ACTIVATION ENERGY (Ea) is the MINIMUM energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur.
Even exothermic reactions need activation energy to get started — you must 'invest' some energy to break existing bonds before the reaction can release more energy.
Example: petrol is stable at room temperature (collisions between O₂ and petrol molecules don't have enough energy). You need a spark (to provide the activation energy) to start the combustion.
EA AND REACTION RATE:
LOW activation energy → many particles have enough energy → FAST reaction.
HIGH activation energy → few particles have enough energy → SLOW reaction.
BOLTZMANN DISTRIBUTION (energy of particles):
At any temperature, particles have a range of energies.
Most particles have intermediate energy — very few have very low or very high energy.
Raising temperature shifts this distribution — more particles move into the high-energy tail (above Ea).
Even a 10°C rise significantly increases the proportion of particles above Ea.

Successful vs Unsuccessful Collisions

Only a small fraction of collisions actually result in reaction:
For a collision to be SUCCESSFUL (effective):
Energy ≥ activation energy → sufficient energy to break existing bonds.
Correct orientation → molecules must collide in the right way for bonds to break and reform correctly.
For example, in the reaction A-B + C → A + B-C:
The C particle must approach the B end of A-B — not the A end.
A collision at the wrong angle gives an unsuccessful result even if energy is sufficient.
This is why not ALL collisions — even energetic ones — lead to products.
INDUSTRIAL RELEVANCE:
Industrial chemists balance:
HIGH TEMPERATURE (faster rate but more energy cost).
CATALYST (lowers activation energy → faster rate without extra heat).
HIGH PRESSURE (for gas reactions — more collisions).
CONCENTRATION (higher concentrations mean more collisions).
⚠️ Common Mistake

Increasing concentration or surface area increases the FREQUENCY of collisions but does NOT change the activation energy. Only a CATALYST changes the activation energy. Temperature increases both frequency AND the proportion above activation energy. These are different mechanisms.

📐 Variables
EaActivation energy (Ea) is measured in kJ/mol (kJ/mol)
📌 Key Note

Collision theory: reaction needs collision + sufficient energy (≥ Ea) + correct orientation. Activation energy = minimum energy for reaction. Low Ea = fast reaction. High Ea = slow reaction. Temperature raises proportion of particles above Ea — more effective collisions. Catalyst lowers Ea.
