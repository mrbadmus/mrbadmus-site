Chemical Bonds

Spec 5.2.1.1 📗 Foundation
📖 In-Depth Theory

Atoms form chemical bonds to reach a more stable arrangement — usually a full outer shell, like the noble gases.

🔻Metals lose
Group 1, 2 and 3 metals give away their outer electrons.
🔺Non-metals gain
Group 5, 6 and 7 non-metals take electrons in.
🤝Non-metals share
Two non-metals share electrons between them.
🧂Ionic
Between which atomsMetal + non-metal
What happens to electronsTransferred, metal → non-metal
ExampleSodium chloride, NaCl
💧Covalent
Between which atomsNon-metal + non-metal
What happens to electronsShared in pairs
ExampleWater, H₂O
🔩Metallic
Between which atomsMetal atoms (pure metal)
What happens to electronsDelocalised into a shared sea
ExampleCopper, Cu
What the atoms ARE decides how their electrons behave — and that is the bond.
🌡️Ionic → high melting point
Strong forces between ions; conducts only when molten or dissolved.
❄️Simple covalent → low melting point
Weak forces between whole molecules are overcome easily.
⚡Metallic → conducts
Delocalised electrons carry charge and heat; metals bend, not shatter.
📌 Key Note

Metal + non-metal → ionic (electron transfer). Non-metal + non-metal → covalent (electron sharing). Metal → metallic (electron sea). Bond type determines properties: ionic = high MP, conducts when molten/dissolved; simple covalent = low MP; metallic = conducts, malleable.

⭐Examiner Tip
When a question says "predict" or "identify" the bonding, justify it from the elements: metal + non-metal → ionic, two non-metals → covalent, a metal alone → metallic. Naming the bond type without saying why the elements produce it rarely earns full marks.
