🧪 Hydrocarbons — Alkanes and Alkenes

Spec 4.7.1 📗 Foundation
📖 In-Depth Theory

Crude Oil and Hydrocarbons

Crude oil = mixture of hydrocarbons (C and H only).

Separated by fractional distillation — different fractions condense at different temperatures.

Shorter chains: lower bp, more flammable, less viscous, more useful as fuels.

Longer chains: higher bp, more viscous, less flammable, used in tarmac and lubricants.

Alkanes — Saturated Hydrocarbons

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. Single bonds only — SATURATED.

Methane (CH₄), ethane (C₂H₆), propane (C₃H₈), butane (C₄H₁₀).

Complete combustion: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O.

Incomplete combustion (limited O₂): produces CO (toxic) + carbon soot.

Alkenes — Unsaturated Hydrocarbons

General formula: CₙH₂ₙ. Contains a C=C double bond — UNSATURATED.

Ethene (C₂H₄), propene (C₃H₆).

More reactive than alkanes — double bond available for addition reactions.

Test: bromine water — decolourised by alkenes (orange → colourless). Alkanes: no reaction.

Cracking

Long-chain alkanes cracked → shorter alkanes + alkenes.

Thermal cracking: 700–1200°C, no catalyst.

Catalytic cracking: ~500°C, aluminium oxide catalyst.

Important because demand for short-chain fuels (petrol) exceeds supply from distillation.

Alkenes produced are used for making polymers (plastics).

⚠️ Common Mistake

Alkanes are SATURATED (single bonds only). Alkenes are UNSATURATED (C=C double bond). The bromine water test distinguishes them: alkenes decolourise it, alkanes don't.

📐 Key Equations
Alkanes: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
Alkenes: CₙH₂ₙ
📌 Key Note

Bromine water: decolourised = alkene (unsaturated). Stays orange = alkane (saturated).
