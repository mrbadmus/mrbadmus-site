In-Depth Theory

What Is a Giant Covalent Structure?

Some covalent substances are not small molecules at all — they are giant covalent structures: huge networks where every atom is joined to its neighbours by strong covalent bonds.

BECAUSE IT'S ONE GIANT NETWORK, IT IS ALWAYS:
🔥Very high melting point
Melting means breaking millions of strong covalent bonds at once — that needs a huge amount of energy.
💎Hard and rigid
Bonds run in every direction through the crystal, locking every atom firmly in place.
🧊Solid at room temperature
The strong network never loosens, so giant covalent substances are always solids.
Not all covalent substances are giant.
Most are simple molecules (H₂O, CO₂) with low melting points. Only the giant networks melt high — always say which type you mean.
Diamond vs Graphite

Both are pure carbon. Every difference in their properties comes from one thing: how the atoms are arranged.

💎
Diamond
BONDS PER CARBON4 — to four other carbons
STRUCTURERigid 3-D tetrahedral lattice
HARDNESSVery hard
MELTING POINTVery high — must break the whole rigid lattice
CONDUCTS ELECTRICITYNo — no free electrons
✏️
Graphite
BONDS PER CARBON3 — one electron left over
STRUCTUREFlat hexagonal layers
HARDNESSSoft & slippery — layers slide
MELTING POINTVery high — layer bonds are strong
CONDUCTS ELECTRICITYYes — 1 delocalised electron per carbon
Same element. Different structure → opposite properties.
Why a Pencil Writes

Graphite’s softness is a process, not a mystery — three steps:

1You press the pencil onto the paper — a force acts on the graphite.
2Only weak forces hold the layers together — no covalent bonds to break.
3Whole layers slide off onto the page — that grey line is sheets of carbon.
🛠️Where each one earns its living
Diamond → cutting tools and drill tips (very hard).
Graphite → pencils and lubricants (layers slide).
Graphite → electrodes in electrolysis (conducts + very high melting point).
Not Just Carbon — Silicon Dioxide

Giant covalent structures can contain more than one element. The exam favourite is silicon dioxide.

🧪Example — silicon dioxide (SiO₂, silica)
The main compound in sand and quartz.
Every silicon atom bonds to 4 oxygen atoms; every oxygen bridges 2 silicons.
Result: one giant lattice → hard, very high melting point, does not conduct.
⚠️Common MistakeSPOT THE ERROR
A STUDENT WROTE:
“Graphite is soft, so its bonds must be weak — it should melt easily.”
What is the flaw in this reasoning?
✅The correction

The covalent bonds within each layer are strong — graphite’s melting point is very high. Only the forces between layers are weak. Weak between-layer forces explain the softness; strong in-layer bonds explain the melting point. Keep the two separate in your answer.
📌

Key Note

Giant covalentevery atom covalently bonded into one huge network → very high melting points.
Diamond4 bonds per carbon → very hard, no free electrons, does not conduct.
Graphite3 bonds per carbon → soft sliding layers, 1 delocalised electron each → conducts.
