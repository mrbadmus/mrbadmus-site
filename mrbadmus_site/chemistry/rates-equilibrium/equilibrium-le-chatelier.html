⚡ Equilibrium and Le Chatelier's Principle

Spec 4.6.2 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

Reversible Reactions

A reversible reaction can go both forward and backward: A + B ⇌ C + D.

In a closed system, both directions happen simultaneously.

At equilibrium: rate forward = rate reverse → concentrations stay constant.

Equilibrium is dynamic — reactions are still happening, just at equal rates.

Le Chatelier's Principle

If conditions change, equilibrium shifts to OPPOSE the change.

Increase reactant concentration → shifts right (makes more product).

Increase temperature → shifts in the ENDOTHERMIC direction.

Increase pressure (gases) → shifts to FEWER moles of gas side.

The Haber Process

N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (forward reaction is exothermic, ΔH = −92 kJ/mol)

Temperature: ~450°C — compromise between rate (needs heat) and yield (lower temp favours NH₃).

Pressure: ~200 atm — favours NH₃ side (4 mol gas → 2 mol gas) but is expensive.

Catalyst: iron — speeds equilibrium without shifting position.

Yield: ~15% — unreacted N₂ and H₂ recycled.

Why Not Change Conditions to Maximise Yield?

Lower temperature → more NH₃ at equilibrium BUT too slow to be economical.

Higher pressure → more NH₃ BUT very expensive equipment, safety risk.

450°C and 200 atm are the industrial compromise.

The catalyst is essential — without it, equilibrium would be reached too slowly at 450°C.

⚠️ Common Mistake

A catalyst does NOT change equilibrium position — it speeds up reaching equilibrium but the final ratio of products to reactants is the same. Only temperature and pressure change the equilibrium POSITION.

📌 Key Note

Le Chatelier: changes are opposed. Endothermic forward → increase temp = more product. Fewer gas moles → increase pressure = more product.

⭐ Higher Tier Only

Equilibrium constant (Kc): concentration of products over reactants. Temperature is the ONLY thing that changes Kc.
