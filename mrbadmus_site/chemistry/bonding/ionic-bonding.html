⚡ Ionic Bonding and Ionic Compounds

Spec 4.2.1 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

What is Ionic Bonding?

Ionic bonding happens between a metal and a non-metal.

The metal LOSES electrons → positive ion (cation).

The non-metal GAINS electrons → negative ion (anion).

Opposite charges attract → strong electrostatic force = the ionic bond.

Both ions achieve a full outer electron shell.

The Giant Ionic Lattice

Ions arrange in a regular 3D structure: the giant ionic lattice.

Each positive ion surrounded by negative ions and vice versa.

Thousands of ions in a repeating pattern.

Example: NaCl — each Na⁺ surrounded by 6 Cl⁻ ions.

Properties of Ionic Compounds

High melting/boiling points — strong electrostatic forces need lots of energy to break.

Conduct electricity when MOLTEN or DISSOLVED — ions free to move.

Do NOT conduct when SOLID — ions locked in lattice.

Brittle — layers shift under stress, like charges align and repel → shatters.

Many are soluble in water.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Ionic compounds conduct electricity ONLY when molten or dissolved in water — NOT as solids. In solids, ions are fixed in the lattice. This is the number one exam mistake on ionic bonding.
