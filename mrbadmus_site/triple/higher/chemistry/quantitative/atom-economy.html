Atom Economy

Spec 4.3.3.2 📙 Higher
📖 In-Depth Theory

What Is Atom Economy?

ATOM ECONOMY measures how efficiently atoms from reactants are converted into the desired product — how much of the mass of reactants ends up in the useful product vs waste products.
EQUATION:
Atom economy (%) = (sum of relative formula masses of desired products ÷ sum of relative formula masses of ALL products) × 100
Note: this uses ALL products from the reaction (including waste), not just the desired one.
ALTERNATIVELY:
Atom economy (%) = (Mr of desired product ÷ sum of Mr of all products) × 100
A HIGH atom economy means:
Most reactant atoms end up in the desired product — efficient.
Little waste is produced.
A LOW atom economy means:
Many reactant atoms end up in waste products — inefficient.
More waste to dispose of — environmental and cost concerns.

Calculating Atom Economy

EXAMPLE 1 — high atom economy:
Manufacture of iron from iron(III) oxide:
Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂
Desired product: Fe (Mr = 56). Two atoms of Fe = 112.
All products: 2Fe (112) + 3CO₂ (3 × 44 = 132) → total = 244.
Atom economy = (112 ÷ 244) × 100 = 45.9%
EXAMPLE 2 — maximum atom economy (addition reactions):
Addition polymerisation: nCH₂=CH₂ → (—CH₂—CH₂—)ₙ
ONLY ONE PRODUCT formed — all atoms go into the polymer.
Atom economy = 100%
Reason: addition reactions always have 100% atom economy because ALL reactant atoms form the single product with no by-products.
EXAMPLE 3 — low atom economy:
Production of calcium oxide: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
Mr of CaO = 56. Mr of CO₂ = 44. Total = 100.
Atom economy = (56 ÷ 100) × 100 = 56%
(44% of the mass becomes CO₂ waste)

Why Atom Economy Matters

GREEN CHEMISTRY and SUSTAINABILITY:
High atom economy = less waste = more sustainable process.
Green chemistry aims to design reactions with high atom economy.
ECONOMIC BENEFITS:
Fewer raw materials wasted → lower costs.
Less waste to dispose of → lower disposal costs.
ENVIRONMENTAL BENEFITS:
Less waste produced → fewer by-products to release or treat.
More sustainable use of raw materials.
COMPARING ROUTES:
Chemists choose synthetic routes with higher atom economy when possible.
Two routes to the same product may have very different atom economies.
ATOM ECONOMY vs PERCENTAGE YIELD:
These are different measures:
Atom economy: a property of the EQUATION — how efficient the chemistry is.
Percentage yield: a property of how the EXPERIMENT is carried out — how much is actually obtained.
Both should be maximised for efficient, sustainable chemistry.
ADDITION vs SUBSTITUTION reactions:
Addition reactions (one product only): atom economy = 100%.
Substitution/elimination reactions (multiple products): lower atom economy.
⚠️ Common Mistake

Atom economy uses the relative formula masses of the PRODUCTS (from the equation) — not the reactants. Divide the Mr of the DESIRED product by the sum of Mr of ALL products. Do not confuse with percentage yield — atom economy is about the equation; percentage yield is about the actual experiment.

📌 Key Note

Atom economy = (Mr desired product ÷ sum Mr all products) × 100. High atom economy = less waste = sustainable. Addition reactions: 100% atom economy (one product). Low atom economy = more waste by-products. Distinct from % yield — atom economy is a property of the reaction equation.
