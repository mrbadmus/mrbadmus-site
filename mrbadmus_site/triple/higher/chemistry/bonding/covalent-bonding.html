🧪 Covalent Bonding and Structures

Spec 4.2.2 📙 Higher
📖 In-Depth Theory

What is Covalent Bonding?

Covalent bonding occurs between non-metal atoms.

Atoms SHARE pairs of electrons to achieve full outer shells.

Each shared pair = one covalent bond.

Double bond = 2 shared pairs (e.g. O=O, C=O in CO₂).

Covalent bonds within molecules are very strong.

Simple Molecular Substances

Small molecules: H₂, O₂, H₂O, CO₂, CH₄, NH₃, Cl₂.

Strong covalent bonds WITHIN molecules.

Weak intermolecular forces BETWEEN molecules.

Low melting/boiling points — weak between-molecule forces easy to overcome.

Do NOT conduct electricity — no free ions or electrons.

Giant Covalent Structures

Diamond: each C bonded to 4 others → 3D network → extremely hard, very high mp, doesn't conduct.

Graphite: each C bonded to 3 others in flat layers → slippery, DOES conduct (delocalised electrons between layers).

Silicon dioxide (SiO₂, sand): giant structure, very high mp, doesn't conduct.

Metallic Bonding

Metal atoms lose outer electrons → positive ions in a sea of delocalised electrons.

The electrostatic attraction between ions and electrons = metallic bond.

Properties: conducts electricity (free electrons), malleable (layers slide), high melting points (strong bonds), shiny.

Alloys: mixing metals distorts the lattice → harder, stronger than pure metals.

⚠️ Common Mistake

NOT all covalent substances have low melting points! Simple molecular compounds do — but giant covalent structures (diamond, SiO₂) have VERY high melting points. The key question is always: simple molecular or giant covalent?

📌 Key Note

Graphite conducts electricity — it has delocalised electrons between layers. Diamond does not conduct — no free electrons.
