Percentage Yield

Spec 4.3.3.1 📗 Foundation
📖 In-Depth Theory

Theoretical Yield vs Actual Yield

In any chemical reaction, the amount of product you calculate from the equation is the THEORETICAL YIELD — the maximum possible amount.
The ACTUAL YIELD is the amount of product you actually collect in the lab.
In practice, actual yield is almost always LESS than theoretical yield.
REASONS FOR LOWER ACTUAL YIELD:
1. REVERSIBLE REACTIONS — the reaction may not go to completion; products form reactants again.
2. SIDE REACTIONS — reactants may form unexpected by-products, using up starting materials.
3. PRACTICAL LOSSES — product sticks to glassware, lost during filtration, evaporation or transfer.
4. IMPURE REACTANTS — some reactants may not react if they contain impurities.
5. INCOMPLETE REACTIONS — insufficient time, temperature or contact between reactants.

Calculating Percentage Yield

EQUATION:
Percentage yield (%) = (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100
Where:
Actual yield = mass of product actually obtained (g)
Theoretical yield = maximum possible mass calculated from the equation (g)
EXAMPLE:
Theoretical yield of calcium carbonate = 50 g
Actual yield collected = 38 g
Percentage yield = (38 ÷ 50) × 100 = 76%
A percentage yield of 100% would mean no product was lost — impossible in practice.
A high percentage yield is desirable — means less waste and lower costs.
INDUSTRIAL IMPORTANCE:
Chemical manufacturers aim to maximise yield to:
Reduce waste and environmental impact.
Reduce costs — fewer raw materials wasted.
Improve profitability of the process.

Why Maximising Yield Matters

ECONOMIC REASONS:
A low yield means raw materials are wasted — higher production costs.
Manufacturers need high yields to make processes economically viable.
ENVIRONMENTAL REASONS:
Wasted reactants may be polluting or energy-intensive to produce.
High yield means less waste entering the environment.
SUSTAINABILITY:
Raw materials are often finite resources — minimising waste conserves them.
EXAMPLE — Haber process:
The reaction N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ is reversible.
At equilibrium, only about 15% conversion to ammonia per pass.
Unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen are recycled — overall yield improved to ~98%.
PRACTICAL TIP — improving yield:
Ensure complete reactions: sufficient time, temperature, catalyst.
Minimise product loss during separation and purification.
Recycle unreacted starting materials where possible.
⚠️ Common Mistake

The theoretical yield must be calculated from the balanced equation — not just assumed. Use moles: find moles of limiting reactant → use molar ratio → calculate mass of product. Then divide actual mass by this calculated theoretical mass.

📐 Key Equations
Percentage yield (%) = (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100
📌 Key Note

% yield = (actual ÷ theoretical) × 100. Always less than 100% in practice. Reasons: reversible reactions, side reactions, practical losses, impurities. High yield = less waste, lower cost, more sustainable. Chemistry-only spec point.
