⚡ Aerobic and Anaerobic Respiration

Spec 4.4.2 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

Aerobic Respiration

Glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy as ATP).

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP.

Occurs in MITOCHONDRIA.

Releases large amount of energy — most efficient method.

Occurs continuously in all living cells, day and night.

Anaerobic Respiration

Respiration WITHOUT oxygen — releases much less energy.

In animals/bacteria: glucose → lactic acid + small amount of ATP.

In yeast/plants: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ + small amount of ATP (fermentation).

Yeast fermentation is used for making bread (CO₂ makes dough rise) and alcohol (ethanol).

Oxygen Debt

During intense exercise, not enough O₂ reaches muscles → anaerobic respiration → lactic acid builds up.

Lactic acid causes muscle fatigue and pain.

After exercise: continue breathing hard — extra O₂ oxidises lactic acid to CO₂ and water.

This extra O₂ needed = oxygen debt (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption, EPOC).

Response to Exercise

Heart rate increases — delivers more O₂ and glucose to muscles.

Breathing rate and depth increase — takes in more O₂, removes more CO₂.

Blood flow redirected to muscles.

After prolonged exercise: glycogen (stored glucose in muscles and liver) depleted.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Anaerobic respiration in ANIMALS produces LACTIC ACID. Anaerobic respiration in YEAST produces ETHANOL + CO₂. These are different products — students often mix them up!

📐 Key Equations
Aerobic: glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water
Anaerobic (animals): glucose → lactic acid
Anaerobic (yeast): glucose → ethanol + carbon dioxide
📌 Key Note

Aerobic = lots of ATP, needs O₂. Anaerobic = little ATP, no O₂ needed. Both start with glucose.
