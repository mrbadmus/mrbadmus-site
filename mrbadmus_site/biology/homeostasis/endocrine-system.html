⚡ The Endocrine System and Blood Glucose

Spec 4.5.3 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

The Endocrine System

Hormones = chemical messengers secreted by glands into the blood.

Carried by blood to target organs — slower than nerves but longer-lasting effects.

Pituitary gland: 'master gland' — controls other glands (releases FSH, LH, ADH etc).

Adrenal gland: adrenaline (fight or flight response).

Pancreas: insulin and glucagon (blood glucose control).

Thyroid: thyroxine (metabolism).

Blood Glucose Regulation

After eating: blood glucose RISES → pancreas releases INSULIN.

Insulin: causes liver and muscles to convert glucose → glycogen (storage). Blood glucose falls.

If too low: pancreas releases GLUCAGON → liver converts glycogen back to glucose. Blood glucose rises.

This is negative feedback — maintains blood glucose at ~5 mmol/L.

Diabetes

Type 1 diabetes: pancreas produces little/no insulin (autoimmune). Treated with insulin injections.

Type 2 diabetes: cells become resistant to insulin. Linked to obesity, poor diet.

Treated with: diet, exercise, tablets (sometimes insulin).

Both types cause dangerously high blood glucose if untreated — damage to blood vessels, nerves, kidneys, eyes.

Nervous vs Hormonal Control

Nervous: electrical signals along neurones, fast, short duration, precise target.

Hormonal: chemicals in blood, slower, longer lasting, affects many target organs.

Both work together: e.g. adrenaline (hormonal) prepares the body for emergency while nervous system coordinates the response.

⚠️ Common Mistake

INSULIN causes blood glucose to FALL (glucose → glycogen storage). GLUCAGON causes blood glucose to RISE (glycogen → glucose released). Students often confuse which hormone does which.

📌 Key Note

Insulin: blood glucose too high → falls. Glucagon: blood glucose too low → rises. Negative feedback.
