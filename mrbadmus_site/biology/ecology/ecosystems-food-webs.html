⚡ Ecosystems, Food Webs and Population

Spec 4.7 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

Ecosystems and Communities

Ecosystem: all the living organisms (community) AND non-living factors (abiotic) in an area.

Population: all individuals of one species in an area.

Community: all populations of different species in an area.

Habitat: the place where an organism lives.

Abiotic and Biotic Factors

Abiotic (non-living): temperature, light, water/rainfall, pH, CO₂ and O₂ levels, mineral content of soil.

Biotic (living): food availability, predators, competition, disease, parasites.

Both types affect the distribution and abundance of organisms.

If conditions change, populations change in response.

Food Chains and Webs

Food chain: shows feeding relationships — producer → primary consumer → secondary consumer → tertiary consumer.

Producer: green plant — makes its own food by photosynthesis.

Consumer: animal — eats other organisms.

Predator: hunts prey. Prey: is eaten by predator.

Food web: many interconnected food chains — more realistic picture of ecosystem.

Removing one species can affect many others (cascade effect).

Sampling Techniques

Quadrats: count organisms in a sample area — calculate population size from mean × total area.

Transects: survey along a line through a habitat — show how organisms change with distance.

Mark-recapture: catch, mark, release, recapture — estimate population size.

Formula: N = (first catch × second catch) ÷ number of marked individuals in second catch.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Energy is LOST at each stage of a food chain (as heat, movement, waste). This is why food chains rarely have more than 4-5 levels — not enough energy reaches the top. Less energy = fewer organisms that can be supported.

📐 Key Equations
N = (n₁ × n₂) ÷ m (mark-recapture)
📌 Key Note

Energy transfer between trophic levels is only about 10% efficient — most is lost as heat.

Example — Mark-recapture population estimate

50 woodlice are caught, marked and released. Later, 40 are caught and 8 are marked. Estimate the population.

F

N = (n₁ × n₂) ÷ m

I

N = (50 × 40) ÷ 8 = 2000 ÷ 8

F

No unit conversion needed

A

N = 250 woodlice

⭐ Higher Tier Only

Efficiency of energy transfer = (energy available at next level ÷ energy available at previous level) × 100%. Pyramids of biomass always decrease upward. Pyramids of number can be inverted (e.g. one tree supports many insects).
