📖 In-Depth Theory

DNA and Genes

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is a double helix polymer of nucleotides.

Located in chromosomes in the nucleus.

A gene = a section of DNA that codes for a specific protein.

Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).

Alleles = different versions of the same gene (e.g. blue eye allele vs brown eye allele).

Dominant and Recessive Alleles

Dominant allele (written as capital letter, e.g. B): expressed even if only one copy is present.

Recessive allele (lower case, e.g. b): only expressed if TWO copies are present.

Genotype: the alleles present (e.g. BB, Bb, bb).

Phenotype: the observable characteristic (e.g. brown eyes, blue eyes).

Homozygous: both alleles the same (BB or bb). Heterozygous: different alleles (Bb).

Punnett Squares

A grid used to predict offspring ratios.

Example: Bb × Bb (both parents heterozygous):

Offspring: BB (1), Bb (2), bb (1) → 3:1 ratio of dominant:recessive phenotype.

75% show dominant phenotype. 25% show recessive.

Bb is a 'carrier' — has the recessive allele but doesn't show it.

Inherited Diseases

Cystic fibrosis: caused by recessive allele (f). Affects cell membranes → thick mucus in lungs/gut.

Polydactyly: caused by dominant allele (P). Extra fingers/toes.

Sickle cell anaemia: recessive. Abnormal haemoglobin → sickle-shaped red blood cells.

Genetic testing can identify carriers — ethical considerations around privacy and insurance.

⚠️ Common Mistake

A carrier of a recessive condition (Ff) has the allele but does NOT show the disease — they appear normal but can pass it on. Two carriers have a 25% chance of an affected child. Students often say carriers 'have the disease mildly' — wrong!

📌 Key Note

Dominant (capital) = shown if even one copy. Recessive (lowercase) = only shown if two copies present.
