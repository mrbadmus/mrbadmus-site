⚡ Newton's Laws of Motion

Spec 4.5.6 📗 Foundation
📖 In-Depth Theory

Newton's First Law — The Lazy Law

An object stays at rest OR moves at constant velocity UNLESS acted on by a resultant force.

Think of it as objects being lazy — they don't want to change what they're doing.

No resultant force = no change in motion. Full stop.

This tendency to resist change is called INERTIA.

Newton's Second Law — F = ma

The resultant force on an object equals its mass × acceleration.

Bigger force = bigger acceleration (for the same mass).

Bigger mass = smaller acceleration (for the same force).

A heavy lorry is harder to speed up AND harder to slow down than a small car — same principle.

Newton's Third Law — Action and Reaction

Every action force has an equal and opposite reaction force.

If you push a wall, the wall pushes back on you with equal force.

These forces act on DIFFERENT objects — so they don't cancel out.

Example: rocket engine pushes gas backward → gas pushes rocket forward (equal and opposite).

Inertia and Mass

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in motion.

Mass is a measure of inertia — more mass = harder to accelerate or decelerate.

This is why a bowling ball is harder to throw and harder to stop than a tennis ball.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Newton's Third Law pairs act on DIFFERENT objects. Students think balanced forces and N3 pairs are the same — they're NOT. Balanced forces act on the SAME object (no resultant). N3 pairs act on DIFFERENT objects (always equal and opposite).

Example 1 — Find acceleration

A resultant force of 600 N acts on a 1200 kg car. Calculate the acceleration.

F

a = F ÷ m

F

A

Example 2 — Find force

F
