Covalent Bonding

Spec 5.2.1.4 📗 Foundation
📖 In-Depth Theory

How Covalent Bonds Form

COVALENT BONDING occurs between NON-METAL atoms.
Instead of transferring electrons (as in ionic bonding), non-metals SHARE pairs of electrons.
Each shared pair of electrons counts as ONE COVALENT BOND.
Why sharing works:
Each atom counts the shared electrons as part of its OWN outer shell.
Both atoms achieve full outer shells — without either losing electrons entirely.
The shared electrons are attracted to BOTH nuclei — this attraction holds the atoms together.
Covalent bonds are STRONG — a lot of energy is needed to break an individual covalent bond within a molecule.

Examples of Covalent Molecules

HYDROGEN (H₂):
Each H has 1 electron — needs 1 more for a full shell (2 electrons).
Two H atoms share 1 pair of electrons → single covalent bond → each H now 'has' 2 electrons. ✓
WATER (H₂O):
O has 6 outer electrons — needs 2 more. H has 1 — needs 1 more.
O forms 2 covalent bonds, one with each H atom.
O now has 8 outer electrons (full). Each H has 2 (full). ✓
AMMONIA (NH₃):
N has 5 outer electrons — needs 3 more.
N forms 3 single covalent bonds with 3 H atoms.
METHANE (CH₄):
C has 4 outer electrons — needs 4 more.
C forms 4 single covalent bonds with 4 H atoms.
OXYGEN (O₂):
Each O has 6 outer electrons — needs 2 more.
Two O atoms share 2 pairs of electrons → DOUBLE covalent bond.
NITROGEN (N₂):
Each N has 5 outer electrons — needs 3 more.
Two N atoms share 3 pairs → TRIPLE covalent bond. N₂ is very stable.

Dot-and-Cross Diagrams for Covalent Molecules

DOT-AND-CROSS diagrams show the shared electrons in covalent bonds.
Convention: each pair of electrons in a bond is shown between the two atoms. Lone pairs (non-bonding) are shown on the relevant atom.
H₂: H × H (one shared pair between the two H atoms — single bond)
H₂O:
H—O—H with 2 lone pairs on O.
The 2 bonding pairs give O its full 8, and each H its full 2.
CH₄ (methane):
4 H atoms arranged around central C.
4 single bonds — C has 8 electrons in outer shell. Each H has 2.
CO₂:
O=C=O — two double bonds.
C has 8 outer electrons (two sets of 2 shared). Each O also has 8.
The key rule: every atom in a correctly drawn dot-and-cross diagram should have a FULL OUTER SHELL.
⚠️ Common Mistake

Students often say that covalent bonds must be weak because substances like water have low boiling points. The covalent bonds themselves are actually very strong. The low melting and boiling points come from the weak intermolecular forces BETWEEN whole molecules — when the substance melts, those weak forces are overcome, not the strong covalent bonds inside each molecule.

📌 Key Note

Covalent bonding: non-metals share electron pairs. Each shared pair = one covalent bond. Both atoms achieve full outer shells. Single bond: 1 shared pair. Double bond: 2 shared pairs. Covalent bonds within molecules are STRONG. Intermolecular forces between molecules are WEAK → low melting points.

⭐ Examiner Tip

Never call covalent bonds weak. To explain a low boiling point, the marks are for the weak intermolecular forces between molecules being overcome — the covalent bonds inside each molecule are strong and stay intact.
