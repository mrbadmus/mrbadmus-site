🌿 Enzymes and the Digestive System

Spec 4.2.1 📙 Higher
📖 In-Depth Theory

What are Enzymes?

Enzymes are biological catalysts — proteins that speed up chemical reactions.

Each enzyme has an active site — a specific shape that fits one substrate (lock and key model).

Lower activation energy → faster reaction.

Denatured enzyme: active site shape changes permanently → no longer works.

Caused by high temperature or extreme pH.

Effect of Temperature on Enzymes

Low temperature: slow reaction — particles have less energy, fewer successful collisions.

Optimum temperature: maximum rate — active site and substrate fit perfectly, many collisions.

Above optimum: enzyme denatures — active site shape distorted permanently.

Human enzymes: optimum ~37°C (body temperature). Plant enzymes may differ.

Effect of pH on Enzymes

Each enzyme has an optimum pH.

pH changes the shape of the active site by affecting hydrogen bonds.

Wrong pH → active site distorted → enzyme less effective → denaturation.

Pepsin (stomach): works best at pH ~2 (acidic). Amylase (mouth): works best at pH ~7 (neutral).

The Digestive System

Mouth: amylase digests starch → maltose.

Stomach: pepsin + HCl digests proteins → peptides. pH ~2.

Small intestine: lipase digests fats → fatty acids + glycerol. Protease + amylase from pancreas.

Bile (from liver, stored in gall bladder): emulsifies fats — breaks large fat droplets into smaller ones → more surface area for lipase.

Large intestine: absorbs water. Rectum: stores faeces.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Enzymes are DENATURED at high temperature — NOT killed. They are proteins whose shape changes permanently. Also — bile EMULSIFIES fats; it doesn't chemically digest them. Lipase does the actual chemical digestion.

📌 Key Note

Lock and key model: enzyme active site (lock) + specific substrate (key) → enzyme-substrate complex → products released → enzyme reused.
