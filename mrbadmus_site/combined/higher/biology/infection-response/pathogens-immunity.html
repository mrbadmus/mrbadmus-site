🌿 Pathogens, Disease and the Immune System

Spec 4.3 📙 Higher
📖 In-Depth Theory

Types of Pathogen

Bacteria: single-celled prokaryotes — reproduce rapidly inside body, produce toxins.

Viruses: not living cells — inject genetic material into host cells, replicate using host machinery.

Fungi: e.g. athlete's foot — grow on/in host tissue.

Protists: single-celled eukaryotes — e.g. Plasmodium causes malaria (spread by mosquito).

How Pathogens Spread

Water: cholera — contaminated drinking water.

Air: influenza, COVID-19 — droplets from coughs/sneezes.

Direct contact: rose black spot fungus on roses — direct touch.

Vectors: malaria — Plasmodium transmitted by Anopheles mosquito bite.

Sexual contact: HIV — transmitted in bodily fluids.

The Immune System

Physical barriers: skin, mucus in nose/airways, cilia.

Phagocytosis: white blood cells (phagocytes) engulf and digest pathogens.

Antibody production: lymphocytes produce specific antibodies that match pathogen antigens.

Memory cells: remain after infection — rapid response if same pathogen returns → immunity.

Vaccination

Vaccine contains dead/weakened/parts of pathogen → triggers immune response without disease.

Memory cells produced → rapid antibody response if real pathogen encountered.

Herd immunity: if enough people are vaccinated, pathogen can't spread easily — even unvaccinated people protected.

Examples: MMR, flu, COVID-19, HPV vaccines.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Antibiotics kill BACTERIA — they have NO effect on viruses. Students often say 'take antibiotics for flu' — this is wrong! Flu is caused by a virus. Antivirals are needed for viral infections (and are much harder to develop).

📌 Key Note

Bacteria: reproduce independently, treated with antibiotics. Viruses: use host cell machinery, NOT treated with antibiotics.
