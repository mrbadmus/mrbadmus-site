⚡ Electrical Power

Spec 4.2.4 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

What is Power?

Power is how quickly energy is transferred.

A 2000 W kettle transfers 2000 J every single second.

A 100 W bulb transfers 100 J every second.

Higher wattage = more energy per second = 'more powerful'.

P = VI — The Main Power Equation

Use this when you know voltage and current.

A higher p.d. means each charge carries more energy.

A higher current means more charge passes every second.

So power goes up when EITHER p.d. or current increases.

P = I²R — When You Don't Know Voltage

Use this when you know current and resistance but not p.d.

Notice the I² — this means doubling the current QUADRUPLES the power.

This is why cables heat up — even a small increase in current causes a BIG increase in power lost as heat.

This is also why the National Grid uses HIGH voltage and LOW current — to reduce I²R losses in the cables.

The National Grid

Electricity travels across the UK at 400,000 V — step-up transformers boost the voltage.

High voltage = low current for the same power.

Low current = less power wasted as heat in the cables (P_loss = I²R).

Before it reaches your home, step-down transformers bring it back to 230 V.

⚠️ Common Mistake

In P = I²R, students forget to square the current first. Write it out as P = (I × I) × R if it helps. Also — don't mix up P = VI with E = QV. Power (P) is energy per second. Energy (E) is power × time.

📐 Variables
PPower (P) is measured in Watts (W)
VPotential Difference (V) is measured in Volts (V)
ICurrent (I) is measured in Amperes (A)
RResistance (R) is measured in Ohms (Ω)
📐 Key Equations
P = VI
P = I²R
⚽ FIFA Worked Examples
Example 1 — Using P = VI

A hairdryer operates at 230 V and draws a current of 5 A. Calculate its power rating.

F

P = V × I

I

P = 230 × 5

F

No unit conversion needed

A

P = 1150 W

Example 2 — Using P = I²R

A resistor of 6 Ω carries a current of 3 A. Calculate the power dissipated.

F

P = I² × R

I

P = 3² × 6 = 9 × 6

F

Square the current FIRST — don't multiply 3 × 6 then square!

A

P = 54 W

Example 3 — National Grid power loss

A transmission cable has resistance 5 Ω and carries a current of 200 A. Calculate the power wasted as heat.

F

P_loss = I² × R

I

P_loss = 200² × 5 = 40,000 × 5

F

Square the current first: 200² = 40,000

A

P_loss = 200,000 W = 200 kW — this is why high voltage (low current) matters!

⭐ Higher Tier Only

National Grid power loss: P_loss = I²R. Doubling transmission voltage halves current, reducing power loss to ONE QUARTER (because I² is quartered). This is why the grid uses 400 kV.
