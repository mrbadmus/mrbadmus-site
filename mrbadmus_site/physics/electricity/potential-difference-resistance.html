⚡ Potential Difference, Current and Resistance

Spec 4.2.1.3 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

What is potential difference (p.d.)?

Potential difference (never call it 'voltage' in physics!) is the energy transferred per unit charge between two points in a circuit. It is the 'push' that drives current around. Measured in volts (V) using a voltmeter in PARALLEL.

What is resistance?

Resistance is the opposition to current flow. Measured in ohms (Ω). A higher resistance means less current flows for the same potential difference. Every component has resistance — even wires (though we usually ignore wire resistance).

Ohm's Law — V = IR

V = potential difference in volts (V). I = current in amperes (A). R = resistance in ohms (Ω). Rearranged: I = V ÷ R and R = V ÷ I. An ohmic conductor obeys Ohm's Law — resistance stays constant as long as temperature is constant.

The I-V graph for an ohmic conductor

A straight line through the origin. The steeper the line, the lower the resistance (more current per volt). Gradient = 1/R. If the line curves, the component is non-ohmic (resistance is changing).

📐 Key Equations
V = IR
I = V ÷ R
R = V ÷ I

2. What does it mean for a component to be 'ohmic'?
Correct! An ohmic conductor has constant resistance — giving a straight I-V graph through the origin ✅
Not quite. Ohmic = constant resistance = straight line through origin on I-V graph.
3. Where is a voltmeter always connected?
Correct! Voltmeters connect in parallel to measure p.d. across a component ✅
Not quite. Voltmeters always connect in PARALLEL across the component being measured.
