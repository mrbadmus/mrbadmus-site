⚡ Gas Pressure and Temperature

Spec 4.3.3 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

How Gas Pressure is Created

Gas particles are constantly moving in all directions.

They collide with the walls of their container billions of times per second.

Each collision exerts a tiny force on the wall.

Pressure = total force from all collisions ÷ area of the walls.

More collisions per second = higher pressure.

Temperature and Pressure (constant volume)

Heat a gas → particles move faster → harder, more frequent collisions with walls → higher pressure.

Cool a gas → slower particles → softer, less frequent collisions → lower pressure.

At absolute zero (−273°C = 0 K), particles would stop completely — no collisions, zero pressure.

This is why pressure is proportional to Kelvin temperature (not Celsius!).

Volume and Pressure (constant temperature)

Compress a gas into a smaller volume → same particles, less space → more collisions per unit area → higher pressure.

Expand a gas into a larger volume → more space → fewer collisions per unit area → lower pressure.

Boyle's Law: pressure × volume = constant (at constant temperature).

The Kelvin Temperature Scale

Kelvin is the absolute temperature scale — starts at absolute zero.

0 K = −273°C. 273 K = 0°C. 373 K = 100°C.

Convert: K = °C + 273.

You MUST use Kelvin (not Celsius) in gas law calculations — otherwise your maths will be wrong!

⚠️ Common Mistake

ALWAYS convert temperature to Kelvin (add 273) before using p/T = constant. Using Celsius gives wrong answers. Also — Boyle's Law only applies at CONSTANT TEMPERATURE. Don't use it if temperature is changing.

📐 Variables
pPressure (p) is measured in Pascals (Pa)
VVolume (V) is measured in Cubic metres (m³)
TTemperature (T) is measured in Kelvin (K)
📐 Key Equations
pV = constant (Boyle's Law)
p₁V₁ = p₂V₂
p/T = constant
p₁/T₁ = p₂/T₂
📌 Key Note

K = °C + 273. Absolute zero = 0 K = −273°C.

⚽ FIFA Worked Examples
Example 1 — Boyle's Law

A gas occupies 4 m³ at 100,000 Pa. The volume is compressed to 2 m³ at constant temperature. What is the new pressure?

F

p₁V₁ = p₂V₂

I

100,000 × 4 = p₂ × 2. So p₂ = 400,000 ÷ 2

F

No unit conversion needed

A

p₂ = 200,000 Pa

Example 2 — Pressure-Temperature Law

A gas at 27°C has pressure 100,000 Pa. It is heated to 127°C at constant volume. Find the new pressure.

F

p₁/T₁ = p₂/T₂ — MUST convert to Kelvin first

I

T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K. T₂ = 127 + 273 = 400 K. p₂ = p₁ × T₂/T₁ = 100,000 × 400/300

F

p₂ = 133,333 Pa

A

p₂ ≈ 133,000 Pa (133 kPa)

⭐ Higher Tier Only

Full combined gas law: p₁V₁/T₁ = p₂V₂/T₂. This applies when all three variables change simultaneously.
