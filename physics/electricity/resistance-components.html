⚡ Resistance — Components and Their Characteristics

Spec 4.2.1.4 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

Ohmic conductor (fixed resistor)

Resistance stays constant as long as temperature stays constant. I-V graph: straight line through origin. Examples: metal wire at constant temp, fixed resistors in circuits.

Filament lamp

As current increases, the filament gets hotter, resistance increases. I-V graph: curves — starts steep then flattens (less current per extra volt). Shape = S-curve. The lamp glows because electrons transfer energy to the filament by heating.

Diode

Only allows current in ONE direction (forward bias). In reverse, resistance is almost infinite — no current flows. Forward threshold ≈ 0.7 V before current flows. I-V graph: flat (reverse), then sharp curve upward (forward). Used in rectification (converting AC to DC).

Thermistor (NTC)

NTC = Negative Temperature Coefficient. As temperature INCREASES → resistance DECREASES. Used in: thermostats, temperature sensors, overheat protection. Real-world: the sensor in your phone that detects temperature.

LDR (Light-Dependent Resistor)

As light intensity INCREASES → resistance DECREASES. Used in: automatic street lights (turn on in dark), phone screen brightness sensors. In a potential divider: bright light → LDR resistance drops → less p.d. across LDR → more p.d. available for other components.

📐 Key Equations
V = IR (for each component)
Vout = Vin × R2/(R1+R2) (Higher — potential divider)

⚽ FIFA Worked Example

A thermistor has a resistance of 2000 Ω at 20°C and 200 Ω at 80°C. The supply p.d. is 6 V. Calculate the current at each temperature.

F

I = V ÷ R

I

At 20°C: I = 6 ÷ 2000 = 0.003 A. At 80°C: I = 6 ÷ 200 = 0.03 A

F

No conversion needed

A

At 20°C: I = 0.003 A (3 mA). At 80°C: I = 0.03 A (30 mA). Higher temp → lower R → more current ✅

⭐ Higher Tier

In a potential divider with an LDR or thermistor: Vout = Vin × R2 ÷ (R1 + R2). As LDR resistance drops (bright light), if LDR is R2, Vout drops. Useful for automatic switching circuits.

🎯 Test Yourself
1. As temperature increases, what happens to a thermistor's resistance?
Correct! NTC thermistors decrease in resistance as temperature rises ✅
Not quite. NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient) thermistors: ↑ temperature → ↓ resistance.
2. What shape is the I-V graph for a filament lamp?
Correct! The filament lamp gives an S-curve because resistance increases as it heats up ✅
Not quite. Filament lamp = S-curve (resistance increases with temperature). Straight line = ohmic resistor. One-direction curve = diode.
3. An LDR is placed in a dark room. What happens to its resistance?
Correct! Less light → higher resistance in an LDR ✅
Not quite. LDR: ↓ light → ↑ resistance. It's the opposite of a thermistor — but the same principle applies in reverse.
