⚡ Resistance — How Components Behave

Spec 4.2.1.4 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

The Ohmic Conductor — Mr Consistent

An ohmic conductor is like a well-trained waiter — no matter how busy the burger van gets, he always delivers at the same rate per burger.

Resistance stays constant at constant temperature.

I-V graph: a perfectly straight line through the origin.

Example: a fixed resistor.

The Filament Lamp — Slows Down When Tired

As current increases, the filament wire gets hotter.

Hotter wire = electrons collide more with the vibrating ions = more resistance.

So the lamp gets harder to push current through as it heats up.

I-V graph: starts steep (easy to push current through when cold), then curves and flattens (harder when hot).

Shape = a gentle S-curve.

The Diode — One-Way Street

A diode is like a turnstile — current can only go one way.

Forward direction: almost no resistance once p.d. exceeds about 0.7 V.

Reverse direction: almost infinite resistance — no current flows at all.

I-V graph: flat line in reverse, then shoots up steeply in the forward direction.

Used to convert AC to DC.

The Thermistor — Hot and Helpful

NTC = Negative Temperature Coefficient.

As temperature INCREASES → resistance DECREASES.

Think: when the burger van heats up, the congestion mysteriously clears!

Used in: thermostats, temperature sensors, phone overheating protection.

The LDR — Lights Up When Needed

LDR = Light-Dependent Resistor.

As light intensity INCREASES → resistance DECREASES.

Think: when the sun comes out, more electrons get 'excited' and the current flows more easily.

Used in: automatic street lights (come on in the dark), phone screen brightness sensors.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Students mix up thermistors and LDRs with normal resistors. Remember: BOTH thermistors and LDRs DECREASE in resistance when their trigger (heat/light) INCREASES. This feels backwards — but it's NTC (Negative Temperature Coefficient) for a reason!

⚽ FIFA Worked Examples
Example 1 — Thermistor current at two temperatures

A thermistor has resistance 2000 Ω at 20°C and 200 Ω at 80°C. The supply p.d. is 6 V. Calculate the current at each temperature.

F

I = V ÷ R (apply separately at each temperature)

I

At 20°C: I = 6 ÷ 2000 = 0.003 A. At 80°C: I = 6 ÷ 200 = 0.03 A

F

No unit conversion needed

A

At 20°C: I = 0.003 A (3 mA). At 80°C: I = 0.03 A (30 mA). Higher temp = lower R = more current ✅

Example 2 — Find resistance from I-V graph data

From an I-V graph, a resistor shows a current of 0.6 A when the p.d. is 3 V. Calculate its resistance and state whether it is ohmic.

F

R = V ÷ I

I

R = 3 ÷ 0.6

F

No unit conversion needed

A

R = 5 Ω. If the I-V graph is a straight line through the origin, it is ohmic.

⭐ Higher Tier Only

In a potential divider with an LDR: as light increases, LDR resistance drops, so the voltage across the LDR drops. If LDR is in position R2: Vout = Vin × R2 ÷ (R1 + R2).
