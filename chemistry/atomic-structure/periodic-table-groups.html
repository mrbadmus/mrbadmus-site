⚡ Groups in the Periodic Table

Spec 4.1.2 Paper 1
📖 In-Depth Theory

Group 1 — The Alkali Metals

Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs — all have 1 outer electron.

React with water → metal hydroxide + hydrogen gas.

Reactivity INCREASES going down (outer electron further from nucleus, easier to lose).

Soft metals, low melting points, float on water.

K catches fire on water. Cs explodes!

Group 7 — The Halogens

F, Cl, Br, I — all have 7 outer electrons, need 1 more for full shell.

Reactivity DECREASES going down (harder to attract an extra electron as atomic radius increases).

More reactive halogen displaces less reactive one from solution.

Appearance: Cl₂ = green gas, Br₂ = brown liquid, I₂ = grey-black solid.

Group 0 — Noble Gases

He, Ne, Ar, Kr — full outer shells, extremely unreactive.

Monatomic (single atoms, not molecules).

Boiling points increase going down the group.

Uses: He in balloons (non-flammable), Ar in light bulbs (inert), Ne in neon signs.

Transition Metals

Central block — Fe, Cu, Ni, Cr etc.

High melting points, hard and dense.

Form coloured compounds (unlike Group 1).

Can have variable oxidation states: Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺, Cu⁺ and Cu²⁺.

Good catalysts: Fe in Haber process, Pt in catalytic converters.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Group 1 reactivity INCREASES down the group. Group 7 reactivity DECREASES down the group. These are opposite trends and always mixed up in exams!

📐 Key Equations
2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂
Cl₂ + 2KBr → 2KCl + Br₂
📌 Key Note

Halogen displacement: more reactive halogen displaces less reactive one. Cl > Br > I in reactivity.
